Principles of Design

Hick's Law

Wolfram Alpha

This is an excellent example of Hick's Law. It does many things, but it doesn't let you know that from the start. You go because you have a question about science or math, and you don't have to go down any series of links to get your answer or scroll down a list of topics. One simply has to type in a question, and the advanced software interprets the question and finds the answer with a surpristing degree of accuracy and completeness. When I go to search for answers on other scientific classroom sites, I generally have t scroll around or search until I come across the correct theme and then onece I have found the theme

Chunking

Twitter

Twitter is the ideal example of chunking because of its 'tweets', where users must state all that they have to say in 140 characters or less. People post links to their articles

Mapping

Mormon.org

This website is so simple and intuitive. It was built to answer questions about probably the most misunderstood church of all time. As such, it was imperative that the website be easy to navigate and use.

Modularity

Google

Google has to be the most developed and complete web service in existence. First known for its search engine, it now has a standardized collaborative online office suite, the largest video publishing site, a pleasantly simple social media outlet, an incomparable maps service and gps navigation program, a beautiful and comprehensive email client, and an extremely effective store for mobile videos, music, books, and applications. These are only a small part of their all-encompassing endeavors, each of these separately developed and maintained, but masterfully intertwined to create the ultimate user experience.
